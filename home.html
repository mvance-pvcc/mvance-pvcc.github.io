The U.S. Department of Agriculture

History

The USDA was signed into law by Abraham Lincoln in 1862. In an message addressing congress, Abraham Lincoln called the USDA “The People's Department”. Since then the USDA has grown in size and scope and continues to serve millions of Americans.

Recent News

On October 27th, 2022 the USDA Secretary unveiled a plan to extend high-speed internet access to rural areas in 24 states and three territories. The plan, named the ReConnect Loan & Grant Program, is to provide $759 million to internet service providers in areas with download and upload speeds of less than 100 megabits per second (Mbps) and 20 Mbps respectively. These internet service providers must commit to constructing the infrastructure required to meet the specified speeds in their respective service areas.

Budget

The USDA requested a total budget of $209 billion for 2023. $169 billion of the 2023 budget is for mandatory services which USDA is required by law to provide. Mandatory budget allocations include programs such as crop insurance, conservation programs, and commodity trade programs. The remaining $39 billion of the budget is discretionary and is used for programs such as rural development grants, national forest management, wild-land firefighting, and much more.

Structure & Mission Areas

The USDA has a responsibility to provide broad array of of services to millions of Americans.To accomplish this, the USDA has broken its responsibilities into eight mission areas which are covered by a multitude of agencies under the USDA's umbrella. Currently, there are eighteen such agencies.

USDA Mission Statement: “To serve all Americans by providing effective, innovative, science-based public policy leadership in agriculture, food and nutrition, natural resource protection and management, rural development, and related issues with a commitment to delivering equitable and climate smart opportunities that inspire and help America thrive.”

Agencies By Mission
Farm Production & Conservation: Provide services tailored to help farmers, ranchers, and other private agricultural land owners. These services include crop insurance, conservation programs, farm safety net programs, agricultural loans, and disaster relief. Farm Service Agency (FSA)
FPAC Business Center
Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS)
Risk Management Agency (RMA)
Food, Nutrition, & Consumer Service: Combat food insecurity, operate nutrition assistance programs, conduct research on nutritional requirements, and provide dietary guidance. Food and Nutrition Service (FNS)
Food Safety: Protect the public by inspecting meat, poultry, and egg products according to food safety standards. Also ensures animals are humanely slaughtered according the the Humane Methods of Slaughter Act. Food Safety and Inspection Service (FSIS)
Marketing & Regulatory Programs: Assist with marketing of agricultural products domestically and internationally. Regulate genetically engineered organisms as well as plant and animal health standards. Agricultural Marketing Service (AMS)
Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service (APHIS)
Natural Resources and Environment: Ensure the country's forests and grasslands remain healthy and divers while meeting the needs of the American people. Forest Service (FS)
Research, Education, and Economics: Conduct research devoted to improving the country's food production system and educate communities on best practices. Agricultural Research Service (ARS)
Economic Research Service (ERS)
National Agricultural Statistics Service (NASS)
National Institute of Food and Agriculture (NIFA)
Rural Development: Invest in rural areas to strengthen the economy and provide diverse opportunities. An example of such an investment is the recently announced ReConnect Loan and Grant Program designed to extend high-speed internet to rural areas. Rural Business-Cooperative Service (RBS)
Rural Development (RD)
Rural Utilities Service (RUS)
Rural Housing Service (RHS)
Trade & Foreign Agricultural Affairs: Ensures American farmers remain competitive in international marketplaces by implementing both domestic and foreign policies. Foreign Agricultural Service (FAS)
